Exponential Family

A family of distributions \(\{\mathcal{P}_{\theta}\}\) is called exponential family if the density function is \[p(x|\theta)=\textrm{exp}(\eta^T T(x)-A(\eta))h(x)\] where \(\eta=\eta(\theta)\in \mathbb{R}^m\) is the natural parameter and \(T(x)\in \mathbb{R}^m\) is the sufficient statistics and \(h(x)\) is the base measure.

Example: The Gaussian distribution \(N(\mu,\sigma^2)\) has density \[p(x|\mu,\sigma)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}\textrm{exp}(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2})\]

where \(\theta=(\mu,\sigma)\), \(T=(x,x^2)^T, \eta(\theta)=(\frac{\mu}{\sigma^2},-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2})\). If \(X_1,\cdots, X_n\) is a random variable sample from \(N(\mu,\sigma^2)\), then the joint density is \[p(x_1,\cdots, x_n|\mu,\sigma)=\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{\frac{n}{2}}}\textrm{exp}(\frac{\mu}{\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^nx_i-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^nx_i^2-n(\frac{\mu^2}{2\sigma^2}+\log\sigma))\] where \(T=(\sum_{i}x_i,\sum_ix_i^2), \eta=(\frac{\mu}{\theta^2},-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2})\).

Example: The Poisson distribution has density \[f(x|\lambda)=\frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^x}{x!}=\textrm{exp}(x\log \lambda-\lambda)\frac{1}{x!}\] where \(\eta=\log \lambda, T=x\).
